the tech thread (or: how i got here without meaning to)

jun 2026

I was thirteen when we got a PC. The first game I remember playing was Shark Attack, barely a game really, just a thing that moved on a screen. But I was hooked immediately on the idea of it. That there was a world inside the machine that I could enter.

By the time I had my own laptop I had played my way through Rollercoaster Tycoon, airport simulators, aquarium managers, and most of the Assassin's Creed series. That last one became something close to a home. I could be anyone in those games. A fashionista, a thief, a treasure hunter, an assassin. I could manage things, build things, make decisions that actually mattered within the rules of the world. Nobody was grading me on it. Nobody was watching me stumble.

I went back to games during law school. Stayed up all night sometimes, which I knew was making things worse, and did it anyway. The guilt and the relief came together. I knew I should be working. I also knew that working meant doing something my brain had quietly decided it would not cooperate with. Games were the only place where I was competent, confident, in control.

Around the same time I tried dual-booting my laptop with Linux. I loved the process of it, more than the result, honestly. The different distributions, the way the system worked, the feeling of being slightly closer to how the machine actually thought. I didn't have enough time to go deep. I was careful not to break anything. Eventually I just deleted it, which felt like a small defeat I didn't tell anyone about.

Then I scored 94.14 percentile on CAT.

It was the first exam I had ever wanted to pass. The first one I had studied for without my brain going quiet and grey. I don't think that's a coincidence. I think when you actually want something, the machinery works differently.

The MBA had data in it. Python, SQL, Power BI, Tableau. Beginner stuff, surface level, but I loved it immediately in the way I had loved dual-booting Linux. Not because I was good at it yet, but because I wanted to be. I kept tinkering after class. I built small things. I broke them and fixed them.

I couldn't give it as much time as I wanted. Grades needed maintaining. The tinkering got quieter.

But it didn't stop.
