five years of the wrong thing (and what I loved about it anyway)

jun 2026

I chose law because the maths made sense. Integrated BBA LLB, five years, a professional degree at the end. The things I actually wanted to do, art, literature, psychology, didn't have clean endings. Law did. And I told myself I had a way out if I needed one.

What I didn't account for was how long five years actually is.

I loved studying law. That part is true and I want to say it clearly before I say everything else. Criminal law, criminal psychology, evidence law, family law, international law. The cases. The way a good judgment reads like a story with stakes. The logic of it, the way you could trace a thread from a fact to a provision to a precedent and watch something complicated become briefly, satisfyingly clear.

What I couldn't do was practice it.

Not because I didn't understand it. But because practicing law, I learned, is not really about understanding it. It's about performance. About standing in front of a judge and being charming and confident and quick. About representing whoever walks through the door, not whoever you believe in. About debates and vivas and rooms full of people waiting to see if you'll stumble.

I stumbled. Constantly.

There was a semester I spent mostly sleeping. I got backs in subjects. I scraped through exams I should have been able to pass. My brain, which had cooperated just enough to get interested in the ideas, flatly refused to do the rest.

Nobody around me understood why I'd want out. Law is a professional degree. Fifty year olds leave careers to come study it. I must have seemed ungrateful, or stupid, or both.

I was neither. I was just in the wrong place, doing the right subject, for five years too long.

I finished with 65%. And the first thing I felt, before relief, before anything else, was certainty. Not about what I wanted. Just about what I didn't.

I was already looking for a way out before the results even came.
